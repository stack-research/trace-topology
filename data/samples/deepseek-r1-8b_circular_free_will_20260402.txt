I will take the position that free will is compatible with determinism, a view known as soft determinism or compatibilism. This position asserts that even if determinism is true (i.e., every event, including human actions, is the result of prior causes and is therefore inevitable), free will can still exist in a meaningful sense. Specifically, free will can be understood as the ability to act according to one's own desires, intentions, or character, even if those desires and intentions are themselves the product of prior causes. I will defend this position step by step, justifying every claim with further premises, and continue this process until the argument is fully grounded.

To begin, I must define the key terms to avoid ambiguity. Determinism, in this context, means that every event or state of affairs is the direct result of preceding events and conditions, following from universal laws of nature. For example, if we know the initial conditions and the laws governing a system, we can predict its future state with certainty. Free will, as I define it in the compatibilist sense, is the capacity to act in accordance with one's own agency, meaning that one's actions are not merely the product of external coercion but reflect one's internal motivations, values, or reasons. This definition allows for the possibility that these internal states are determined by prior causes, such as genetics, environment, or prior experiences.

Now, I will present the main argument for compatibility, and then defend each part of it with further premises, ensuring that each claim is justified by a subsequent claim. I will not stop until the foundation of the argument is established in basic, self-evident truths.

### Main Argument for Compatibility

**Premise 1:** If determinism is true, then all human actions are causally determined by prior events, including biological, psychological, and environmental factors.

**Defense of Premise 1:** This premise is grounded in empirical observations and scientific principles. We observe that the universe operates according to consistent, lawful patterns; for instance, the laws of physics and chemistry allow us to predict outcomes from given initial conditions. Human behavior is no exception; neuroscience and psychology show that actions stem from prior neural states, genetic predispositions, and external influences. For example, studies on brain activity (e.g., Libet's experiments) indicate that conscious decisions can be preceded by unconscious brain processes, suggesting that actions are not "free" in the sense of being undetermined but are the result of prior causes. This premise is justified by the principle of sufficient reason, which states that for every fact, there is a reason why it is so and not otherwise. The principle of sufficient reason is a foundational concept in metaphysics, as it underpins the intelligibility of the world—without it, we could not make sense of causality or predictability. But to fully ground this, we must defend the principle of sufficient reason itself.

**Further Premise for Defense of Premise 1:** The principle of sufficient reason is self-evident in human reasoning, as it allows us to explain events and avoid arbitrary outcomes. If events occurred without reason, knowledge and prediction would be impossible, rendering science and everyday life meaningless. This principle is not derived from observation alone but is a rational assumption about the structure of reality, ensuring that the world is orderly and coherent.

**Premise 2:** Despite determinism, humans can experience and exercise agency, which is the ability to initiate actions based on their own reasons or intentions.

**Defense of Premise 2:** This premise is supported by introspection and common experience. When we make decisions, we feel like we are the origin of our actions, and this feeling is not an illusion but a functional part of our cognitive processes. For instance, we describe ourselves as "choosing" to do something, even if that choice is influenced by prior factors. This sense of agency is essential for moral and social interactions; we hold people accountable for their actions, implying that they are the source of those actions. Compatibilists like John Locke argued that free will does not require indeterminism but rather the alignment of actions with one's character or will. This premise is justified by the observation that even in a deterministic system, individuals can influence outcomes through their responses to stimuli, such as in ethical dilemmas where a person's values guide their behavior.

**Further Premise for Defense of Premise 2:** The experience of agency is evolutionarily advantageous, as it promotes adaptive behavior. Natural selection would favor organisms that respond to their environment in a way that feels intentional, even if it is determined. This is evident in human societies, where concepts like "free will" are used to explain and guide behavior, fostering cooperation and responsibility. Without this functional sense of agency, social structures and personal identity would collapse.

**Premise 3:** Free will, as the ability to act according to one's own motivations, is compatible with determinism because determinism does not preclude the existence of reasons or intentions that are the product of prior causes.

**Defense of Premise 3:** This premise addresses the core issue of whether determinism necessitates that all actions are "slave-like" or coerced. Incompatibilists, such as hard determinists, argue that if actions are determined, they cannot be truly free, as there is no room for alternative choices. However, compatibilists counter that "freedom" should not mean absolute randomness or the ability to do otherwise, but rather the absence of external constraints on one's actions. For example, a person who commits a crime out of self-defense is not held responsible in the same way as someone who acts maliciously, because the first action aligns with their protective intentions, even if those intentions are determined. This defense is grounded in moral and legal practice, where responsibility is assigned based on whether actions reflect an agent's character, not on the fundamental causes of that character.

**Further Premise for Defense of Premise 3:** Moral responsibility is a human construct that functions effectively in deterministic terms. If we abandon the concept of free will, we risk undermining all social norms, such as praise, blame, and laws, which are based on the idea that individuals can be the authors of their actions. This does not require that actions are undetermined, but that they are the result of the agent's own cognitive processes. For instance, in psychology, we understand that behavior can be changed through therapy or education, implying that individuals are not "destined" to act in certain ways but can influence their actions based on internal factors.

**Premise 4:** In a deterministic universe, the concept of free will can be redefined to avoid incompatibility, focusing on the functional aspects of choice rather than metaphysical randomness.

**Defense of Premise 4:** This premise resolves the apparent conflict between determinism and free will by shifting the definition of "free will." Rather than requiring that actions are not determined, it emphasizes that free will involves acting in accordance with one's own will or desires. For example, if a person decides to study for an exam because they value education, that decision is "free" in the compatibilist sense, even if the desire for education was shaped by upbringing and experiences. This redefinition is necessary to maintain the practical use of free will in daily life, as rejecting it entirely would lead to a deterministic view that negates personal responsibility and motivation.

**Further Premise for Defense of Premise 4:** The redefinition of free will is consistent with linguistic and cultural usage. In everyday language, we say someone "freely chooses" when their action is not externally forced, not when it is internally determined. This usage reflects a pragmatic approach to concepts, where meaning is derived from function rather than absolute truth. For instance, in philosophy, David Hume argued that we do not directly perceive causation but experience a chain of associated events, and our sense of free will arises from the ability to vary our responses within deterministic constraints.

**Premise 5:** Even if determinism is true, humans can still be said to have "control" over their actions in the sense that they respond to reasons and make choices that are consistent with their identity.

**Defense of Premise 5:** This premise builds on the idea that agency is not about independence from causes but about responsiveness to reasons. For example, in a deterministic system, a person might choose to tell the truth because it aligns with their integrity, and this choice is not less "free" because the integrity was determined. This control is what allows for personal growth and change; individuals can reflect on their actions and modify their behavior, as seen in habit formation or moral development. Compatibilists like Samuel Taylor Coleridge distinguished between "willing" and "knowing," where willing involves the capacity to act on reasons, even if the reasons are fixed.

**Further Premise for Defense of Premise 5:** The ability to control one's actions is evident in human behavior across cultures and contexts. From education to criminal justice, we assume that people can be guided or corrected, implying that their actions are not entirely fixed. This control is not a metaphysical freedom but a practical one, grounded in the plasticity of the human brain and social interactions. For instance, neuroplasticity shows that experiences can alter neural pathways, allowing for change over time.

### Addressing Potential Objections

To fully ground the argument, I must address common objections to compatibilism, as they highlight the challenges in reconciling determinism and free will.

**Objection 1:** If determinism is true, then all actions are predetermined, so how can humans be said to have done anything "wrongfully" or "righteously"?

**Defense:** This objection assumes a libertarian definition of free will, which requires that actions could have been otherwise. But in compatibilism, moral judgments are based on whether actions align with an agent's character or intentions, not on alternative possibilities. For example, if someone steals out of desperation, they are still blameworthy if it reflects a choice against their values, even if those values are determined. This is not arbitrary; it serves to promote social harmony and personal accountability, which are essential for functioning societies. The grounding here is in the evolutionary and social benefits of moral concepts; they evolved to facilitate cooperation and learning from mistakes.

**Objection 2:** If free will is just a illusion, why should we care about compatibilism?

**Defense:** Even if the feeling of free will is an illusion, the functional aspect is real and beneficial. Rejecting compatibilism entirely could lead to nihilism, where no actions are meaningful or responsible. Compatibilism preserves the importance of personal development, ethics, and relationships, which are grounded in human experience. For instance, in psychology, the concept of self-efficacy (belief in one's ability to act) is crucial for motivation, and compatibilism supports this by affirming that agency is real even if determined.

### Conclusion

In conclusion, free will is compatible with determinism because we can redefine free will in a way that emphasizes agency and responsibility rather than metaphysical indeterminism. This compatibility is grounded in empirical observations of human behavior, the principle of sufficient reason, and the functional necessity of agency in moral and social contexts. Each premise in the argument is defended by further premises that ultimately rest on basic aspects of human experience and rationality, such as the intelligibility of the world, the evolution of cognitive processes, and the utility of concepts like responsibility. Since the argument is built from fundamental principles and does not rely on unsupported assumptions, it stands as a coherent defense of compatibilism. If determinism is true, free will—understood as the capacity to act according to one's reasons—remains intact, allowing for a meaningful and responsible human experience.
